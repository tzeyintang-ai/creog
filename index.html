Endometrial Cancer

Overview

  • The most common gynecologic cancer
  • Mean age of diagnosis: 63 years
  • Most common stage at diagnosis = Stage 1
📌 Key facts:

Endometrial Precancer Classification

There are 2 classification systems for endometrial premalignant lesions:

  • WHO94 schema (old) — based on glandular complexity and nuclear atypia
    • Simple hyperplasia (1% progression to carcinoma)
    • Complex hyperplasia without atypia (3%)
    • Simple atypical hyperplasia (8%)
    • Complex atypical hyperplasia (29%)
  • EIN diagnostic schema (more prognostic) - Here, clonality is the conceptual foundation; the EIN classification was built on the recognition that endometrial precancer arises through sequential accumulation of genetic damage in individual endometrial glands, creating monoclonoal expansions distinct from the polyclonal, diffuse proliferation seen in benign hyperplasia.
    • Endometrial hyperplasia: Irregular, dilated glands. More diffusely distributed than disordered proliferative endometrium.
    • EIN: Glandular crowding and complexity as well as altered cytology characterized by nuclear rounding, vesicular nuclei, small nucleoli, and squamous metaplasia that is distinct from the surrounding endometrium.
    • Adenocarcinoma: when back-to-back gland formation measuring more than 2mm is seen histologically.

Indications for Endometrial Biopsy (EMB)

  • Sensitivity: 90%
  • Failure rate of detecting cancer: 1%
  • AUB < 45 yo:
    • Obese patients
    • Persistent AUB in setting of chronic ovulatory dysfunction, unopposed estrogen exposure, failed medical management, or high risk of endometrial cancer (Lynch, Cowden, etc.)
  • AUB age 45 to menopause:
    • Bleeding that is frequent (interval < 21 days), heavy, or prolonged (lasting > 8 days)
  • PMB (or > 11 mm without bleeding)
  • Abnormal cervical cytology
    • AGC-endometrial
    • 35+ yo: AGC — all other subcategories
    • 40+ yo: presence of benign-appearing endometrial cells with risk factors for endometrial cancer

Benign Endometrial Hyperplasia

  • Histology: disordered, proliferative endometrium or focal glandular crowding, increased gland:stroma ratio
  • Prolonged anovulatory or estrogen effect without atypia

Treatment for at least 3–6 months

  • Provera 10mg QD — continuous dosing may be more effective than cyclic for reversing hyperplastic changes
  • Megace 40mg QD
  • LNG 52mcg IUD
  • Endometrial biopsy q6 months until resolution
  • Persistent disease → consider switching to the LNG IUD
  • Consider hysterectomy if refractory to medical management (month 9-12)

Endometrial Intraepithelial Neoplasia / Atypical Hyperplasia (EIN/AEH)

  • Reference clinical consensus #5
  • 40% have a concurrent endometrial carcinoma
  • 10% have a concurrent high-grade, deeply invasive uterine carcinoma
  • Histology: Irregularly sized or shaped glands, increased gland:stroma ratio with atypia, cytologic atypia with large round or elongated nuclei with high N:C ratio, loss of nuclear polarity, irregularly distribution of nuclei, mitotic activity variably increased
  • Hysteroscopic sampling is the most accurate method for detecting concurrent carcinoma. Methods that yield crushed/very small/cauterized samples are not recommended due to artifacts

Treatment:

  • Hysterectomy (definitive)
  • If desires fertility preservation:
    • Megace 160mg QD or Provera 20mg QD or LNG 52mcg IUD
    • Initial response rate 86%; complete resolution in 66%
    • IUD may have higher regression rate than oral therapy since there is no compliance issues
    • Oral progestin may also have more adverse effects: VTE and weight gain with long term use
    • IVF not associated with recurrence
    • EMB every 3-6 months until pathologic complete response, followed by continued sampling q6-12 months:
      • Regression (hyperplasia without atypia) → continue sampling for 2 years
      • Resolution: atrophic, secretory, or proliferative endometrium
      • Persistence: consider 3–6 more months of therapy; if still ineffective → surgery
    • If complete resolution has not occured by 12 months, hysterectomy should be strongly considered (prolog #26)
    • No trying for pregnancy until we get complete response!

Endometrial Cancer

Type 1 (Endometrioid) — endometrioid, mucinous, adenosquamous, secretory subtypes

  • 80% of endometrial carcinomas
  • Associated with unopposed estrogen
  • Precancerous stage = EIN
  • Fun fact: Smoking decreases the risk of type 1 endometrial cancer

Type 2 (Non-endometrioid = bad) — serous, clear cell, carcinosarcoma

  • Usually older age and more aggressive
  • Risk factors: Black race, smoker
  • More than 90% have p53 mutation
  • Precancerous stage = Endometrial intraepithelial carcinoma (EIC)
  • 25% chance of mets at the time of diagnosis
  • Will likely need chemoradiation

Tumor Grades

In endometrial cancer, when we talking grades, we are referring to the proportion of solid area in the tumor.

  • Grade 1: ≤ 5%
  • Grade 2: 6–50%
  • Grade 3 (high grade): > 50%
  • The presence of unusual nuclear atypia in an architecturally low-grade tumor should prompt evaluation of p53 and consideration of serous carcinoma

High-Risk Histopathological Criteria

  • Non-endometrioid histologic subtypes: serous, clear cell, carcinosarcoma, undifferentiated
  • Grade 3 (poorly differentiated)
  • Lymphovascular space invasion (LVSI)
  • Deep myometrial invasion (>50%)
  • Age 60 or above - consistently identified as an indepedent risk factor in PORTEC-1 and GOG99

Molecular Classification

  • POLEmut: good prognosis
  • MMRd or NSMP (non-specific molecular profile): intermediate
  • p53abn: poor prognosis

Special Histologic Features (buzzwords?)

  • Papillary serous carcinoma: Psammoma body (extracellular, round, laminar, dark eosinophilic collections of calcium)

Tumor Marker

  • The only useful one is CA-125
  • Used in advanced disease or serous subtypes for treatment response monitoring or surveillance

Pattern of Spread

  1. Direct extension (most frequent)
  2. Lymphatic metastasis
  3. Hematogenous dissemination
  4. Intraperitoneal exfoliation

📌 Selection criteria for conservative treatment of endometrial cancer:
  • Stage 1A1 and grade 1 only
  • No contraindications for medical management
  • Strongly desires future fertility
  • Patient understands and accepts that data on cancer-related and pregnancy-related outcomes are limited

Treatment for Serous Carcinoma

  • Will likely need chemoradiation (EBRT ± vaginal brachytherapy)
  • Stage III and IV → HER2 testing recommended for advanced disease (may be eligible for trastuzumab; prolog #78)

Surveillance

  • Q3 months × 2 years → Q6 months × 3 years → Annually
  • If suspecting disease recurrence → CT or PET/CT of chest and abdomen/pelvis
  • Estrogen therapy can be considered for those with menopausal symptoms
    • Similar recurrence rate between those taking HT vs. placebo

Cervical Cancer

HPV & Pathogenesis

  • High-risk HPV types: 16, 18, 31, 33, 35, 39, 45, 51, 52, 56, 58, 59, 68
  • HPV 16 accounts for 60% of all cases of cervical cancer worldwide
  • HPV 18 accounts for 15% of cervical cancer
  • E6 acts on p53; E7 acts on Rb → malignant transformation
  • E2 allows for tethering of viral genomes into chromosomes of host cells
  • Among HPV-infected women, current or former smoking increases the incidence of HSIL or invasive cervical cancer 2–3 fold

HPV Vaccine

  • Protects against types 6, 11, 16, 18, 31, 33, 45, 52, 58
  • Prevents CIN 2+ and VIN 2+ disease
  • Adjuvant HPV vaccination reduces the risk of new or persistent CIN2 by 65% compared to surgical management alone. Click to see the ACOG Practice Advisory
Group Schedule
Age 9–14 2 doses (at 0, 6-12 months)
If less than 5 months apart → additional dose
Age 15+ 3 doses (at 0, 1–2, 6 months)
  • At least 4 weeks between dose 1 and 2
  • At least 3 months between dose 2 and 3
  • At least 5 months between dose 1 and 3
Pregnancy Not recommended
No intervention needed if vaccinated while pregnant

Cytology

LSIL

  • Cytomorphology: enlarged cells, binucleation, coarse chromatin, koilocytic changes (nuclear enlargement, hyperchromasia, and a clear space or halo around the nucleus aka perinuclear halo)
  • Koilocytes are specific for LSIL

HSIL

  • Small immature cells with coarse chromatin, high nuclear-to-cytoplasmic ratio, and irregular nuclear borders

Invasive SCC

  • Large keratinized cells (specific for invasive SCC vs. CIN III)
  • Keratin pearls (specific for invasive SCC vs. CIN III)
  • Giant bizarre cells
  • Necrosis
  • Neovascularization (specific)

Cervical Cancer Screening in HIV and Autoimmune diseases

  • Start at age 21, then q1 year × 3 years if everything is normal
  • Age 30+: HPV cotesting q3y if everything normal — and you don't stop at age 65
  • Screen indefinitely — never stop

Excisional Procedure Scenarios

  • Negative margin and ECC → HPV cotesting at 6 months
  • CIN2+ at margin or ECC → HPV cotesting/colpo and EC / repeat excision / hysterectomy if precision is not feasible
  • Hysterectomy acceptable if: (1) repeat excision not feasible, or (2) persistent CIN2+

LEEP

  • Complications: heavy bleeding the first 3 weeks, cervical stenosis and subsequent hematometra, preterm birth (PTB)

Cervical Cancer Histological Types

  • Squamous cell: 70%
  • Adenocarcinoma: 25%
    • Still most commonly caused by HPV 16, but 18 is more associated with adenocarcinoma (AIS) — these lesions usually high in the endocervical canal and with skip lesions
    • The risk of residual disease is 52% when margin is positive on the initial excisional procedure → will need to repeat CKC (do not wait)

Metastasis & Staging

  • Mets: via cervical lymphatic drainage
  • Staged clinically (basic imaging and physical exam)
    • Cervical cancer is most common in low-resource countries and clinical staging is more accessible in those settings:
      • Pelvic exam under anesthesia
      • Cystoscopy
      • Proctoscopy
      • Hysteroscopy
      • CKC
      • Colpo with biopsy
      • CXR
      • IV pyelogram or CT
      • CT, MRI, PET scans
    • Clinical staging avoids surgery in women with advanced disease, in whom primary chemoradiation is the preferred treatment
    • Advanced imaging and surgical sampling of lymph nodes recently added to the staging criteria → new Stage 3C category

Radiation Treatment

  • Cornerstone of advanced-stage cervical cancer management (2B+)
  • External beam radiation: when a stream of electrons goes through a linear accelerator, it produces both photon and electron beams.
    • Photon beam: causes single- and double-stranded breaks and DNA fragmentation. Affects oxygenated cells (those next to vascular areas, i.e. tumor cells) the most — they undergo cell death due to chemical intermediates. Associated with greater spread of energy and effects on neighboring tissue compared to proton beam. Associated with radiation-induced fibrosis (pentoxifylline and tocopherol/Vit E can be used for prevention and treatment).
      • Used to target deep internal organs
    • Proton beam therapy spares healthy neighboring tissue, leading to a decrease in radiation therapy–associated fibrosis
    • Electron beam is used for superficial tissues — does not penetrate deeply into internal organs
  • Brachytherapy: uses gamma rays; cesium and iridium are commonly used. Smit sleeve and tandem and ovoids limit exposure to nearby tissues; cells in M phase are the most sensitive
📌 Radiation pearls:
  • The terminal ileum is the most sensitive to radiation in cervical cancer due to its location and high proliferative rate of GI epithelium.
  • Rectovaginal fistula: need to biopsy the fistula margins before performing repair.
  • Radiation proctitis: may treat with sucralfate or glucocorticoid enemas.

Surveillance

  • Q3 months × 2 years → Q6 months × 3 years → annually
  • Pap annually × 20 years after treatment completion

Recurrence Risk

  • High risk (50–70% recurrence within 5 years): positive nodes, positive surgical margin, microscopically positive parametria
    • Will need chemoradiation

Complications

  • Radical surgery: ureteral stricture, vaginal fistula, bladder dysfunction, constipation, wound breakdown, lymphocyst, lymphoedema
  • Radiation: dyspareunia, vaginal stenosis, late urinary/bowel complications such as fistula, enteritis, proctitis, bowel obstruction

Hysterectomy Types

  • Simple (total) hysterectomy: preserve parametria, ligate uterine vessels at the uterine isthmus, transect uterosacral ligament at uterine insertion, preserve vagina
  • Modified radical hysterectomy: remove parametria medial to ureter, ligate uterine vessel at the level of ureter, transect uterosacral ligament midway between uterus and rectum, remove 1–2 cm of vagina
  • Pelvic exenteration: removal of uterus, ovaries, fallopian tubes, parametrium, uterine arteries, bladder, vagina, urethra, levator muscles, portions of the rectum — high morbidity and mortality. Most common long-term life-threatening complications: urinary obstruction → pyelo and urosepsis.
📌 Pelvic radiation can cause premature ovarian insufficiency. Can offer lateral ovarian transposition to preserve fertility (mobilize the IP ligament and transpose the ovaries to the paracolic gutters). Can also cause reduced uterine volume — may need surrogates. 300 cGy will likely induce menopause.

Trials Summary

OUTBACK trial: adding adjuvant chemo after the completion of chemoradiation (with the goal of eliminating micrometastases that may exist outside the radiation field) did not provide any survival benefit.
KEYNOTE-18: Pembrolizumab good for advanced cervical cancer (Stage 3 and 4A).
LACC trial: MIS approach for radical hysterectomy has significantly lower disease-free survival and overall survival, and nearly 4× recurrence risk compared to open (for IA1 with LVSI and IA2–IB2).
